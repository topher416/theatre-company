A Founders' Syllabus in Building a Theatre Company

A graduate-level self-directed education designed for two founders who know the art but need to rapidly master the business, the ecosystem, and the strategy. Eight modules. Twenty months. One opening night.

Module 01
How Theatre Companies Are Born
History, DNA, and the patterns of survival

Before building anything, study what has survived and why. The companies that last share common traits: irrational commitment, artistic specificity, fiscal discipline, and the patience to build one audience member at a time. This module grounds your instincts in evidence.

Read

John Mayer — Methuen Drama, 2016
The definitive oral history of how nine actors in a church basement built one of the most important theatres in America. Essential for understanding ensemble culture, artistic identity, and the real economics of early-stage company building. Pay particular attention to how they handled the transition from amateur passion project to professional institution.

Module 02
Inside The Thin Place
The playwright, the text, the production history, the theatrical séance

You must know this play more deeply than anyone who has ever produced it. Its origins, its influences, its production demands, its critical reception, and its relationship to Lucas Hnath's larger body of work. This module makes you the foremost authorities on why this play, why now, why you.

Read
The Thin Place — Script
Lucas Hnath — Published by Concord Theatricals
Read it three times. First for story. Second for structure — how Hnath moves from monologue to dialogue to ensemble scene to the dark sequence. Third for production: what does the play actually need in terms of space, light, sound, and performer-audience relationship? The house lights are up for most of the play. That's a design choice that is also a philosophical choice. Understand why.

Research assignment
The original production used illusion design by Steve Cuiffo, a professional magician and theatre artist. Your license may require use of the original design. Research Cuiffo's work, contact Concord Theatricals about design requirements, and study how magic and mentalism techniques function as theatrical storytelling. Module 03
Building the Organization
Legal structure, revenue diversification, and the machinery of an LLC

This is the module that separates theatre artists who dream from theatre artists who produce. The recommended path is an LLC for operational flexibility and profit distribution, paired with Fractured Atlas fiscal sponsorship for the tax-deductible donation channel. You need to understand LLC formation, operating agreements, fiscal sponsorship mechanics, insurance, and the revenue streams this structure enables — not at expert level, but well enough to make informed decisions and ask the right questions.

Do
Form the Illinois LLC
Action item — target completion: March 2026
File Articles of Organization with the IL Secretary of State ($150 filing fee, $75/year annual report). Choose your company name first, or file under a working name and amend later. The LLC gives you limited liability protection, pass-through taxation, the ability to distribute profit to both founders, and full commercial flexibility. No board of directors required. The Equity CAT Agreement works with both commercial and nonprofit producers — an LLC does not limit your ability to produce Equity theatre.
IL Secretary of State — LLC filing →
Do
Draft the LLC Operating Agreement
Action item — target completion: March 2026
This is the co-founder agreement in legal form. It defines who does what, who decides what, how profits are split, and what happens if one founder wants to leave. Illinois does not require an operating agreement, but operating without one is reckless. Write this together. Cover: roles and responsibilities, decision-making authority, financial contributions, profit and loss allocation, and exit provisions. Consider a one-hour consultation with a lawyer ($150-300) to review it.
Do
Apply to Fractured Atlas
Action item — target completion: March 2026
Apply for Model C fiscal sponsorship through Fractured Atlas ($129/year, 8% admin fee on donations). This gives you the ability to accept tax-deductible donations within two weeks. Fractured Atlas explicitly supports projects whose legal entity is an LLC — the company's for-profit structure does not disqualify you. This channel handles donations and crowdfunding; the LLC handles everything else.
Apply at Fractured Atlas →

Study
Revenue Diversification: What the LLC Enables
Research assignment
The LLC structure enables revenue streams that a nonprofit cannot easily pursue: consulting services for other theatre companies (design, grant writing, literary management), paid workshops and classes, experiential events, and professional services. Map out which revenue streams are realistic given your skills and capacity. Topher's design and communications skills serve a market of 240+ Chicago theatre companies. Cassie's arts administration experience is exactly what understaffed storefronts need. The entity should generate income year-round, not just during production runs.
Founders' Discussion

The LLC operating agreement is the co-founder agreement in legal form. Before you write it: Who is the artistic director? Who is the managing director? Who makes final casting decisions? Who signs the checks? How are profits split? What happens if one founder wants to leave? What happens if you disagree about a play? Have this conversation before you need the answers.

Module 04
The Art of Producing
Equity contracts, licensing, venue relationships, and production management

Cassie has managed contracts and seasons at SLAC. Topher has produced and directed. But producing as the organization — as the entity that bears the legal and financial risk — requires a different kind of mastery. This module fills the gap between "I've worked in theatre" and "I run a theatre."

Read
Stage Money: The Business of the Professional Theater
Tim Donahue & Jim Patterson — USC Press
The most rigorous breakdown of theatrical economics available. Covers budgeting, union contracts, ticket pricing, marketing, and the relationship between commercial and nonprofit sectors. Data-driven, chart-heavy, and unsentimental about the financial realities. Read the chapters on budgeting and nonprofit producing three times.
Find this book →
Read
Open Book Theatre Management
Rafe Beckley — Zer0 Books
Written for low- and no-budget producers who still want to operate ethically. Covers profit-share models, transparent budgeting, cashflow management, and the core principle: you have to make money on your first show to get a second show. The most relevant financial philosophy for where you're starting.
Find this book →
Do
Call AEA Central Regional Office
Action item — target completion: first 30 days
312-641-0393. 557 W Randolph Street, Chicago. This is the single most time-sensitive action item. You must resolve: (1) Can Cassie as an AEA member co-found and produce a company in which she also performs? (2) What are the specific provisions of the CAT Agreement entry tier for a 99-seat house? (3) What documentation is required to register a 29-Hour Staged Reading? Do not proceed past this module until this call is made and the answers are documented in writing.
Study
Actors' Equity — CAT Agreement and 29-Hour Reading Code
Actors' Equity Association
Study both documents in full. The CAT Agreement was renegotiated in March 2024 (new 4-year term). The 29-Hour Code governs your October 2026 reading: max 29 hours over 14 days, max 3 presentations, invited audience only, no admission, no critics, no recording. Violations can result in Equity sanctions that would cripple you before you start.
Actors' Equity Association →
Do
Submit Licensing Inquiry to Concord Theatricals
Action item — target completion: March 2026
Email licensing@concordtheatricals.com. Inquire about: (1) Reading license for October 2026, (2) Full production license for October 2027, (3) Estimated royalty rates for both amateur and professional classifications, (4) Requirements regarding Steve Cuiffo's original illusion design. Be professional. Include your credentials. First impressions with licensors matter.
Founders' Discussion

If Equity says Cassie cannot perform in a company she co-produces under the CAT Agreement, what is Plan B? Discuss three scenarios: she performs non-Equity for the first production, she steps back from performing and directs, or you produce the first show entirely non-Equity. Have this conversation before you make the call.

Module 05
Revenue, Fundraising, and Financial Survival
How to fund the work without losing your soul
"You have to make money first and art second, because if you make money on your first show you are far more likely to be able to stage the second."
Rafe Beckley, Open Book Theatre Management

You've already decided to reject dependence on institutional philanthropy. Good. That decision shapes everything. This module equips you to build a revenue model centered on earned income — ticket sales, micropatronage, and strategic self-sufficiency — while understanding enough about grants and donors to use them when they serve the mission rather than distort it.

Read
Entrepreneurial Arts and Cultural Leadership

Brand new, co-authored by a Carnegie Mellon arts management professor and the founder of Imagination Stage. Real-world strategies for developing an entrepreneurial mindset in nonprofit arts. Covers financial instability navigation, sustainable business models, and what "entrepreneurial" actually means when your product is art. The most current thinking available.

Do
Build Your First Production Budget
Action item — by May 2026
Using the financial models from the founding memorandum as a baseline, build three scenarios: lean ($18K), moderate ($28K), and ambitious ($40K). Identify the go/no-go fundraising threshold — the number below which you do not proceed with the October 2027 production. This decision should be made jointly, documented, and not revisited under pressure.
Do
Write the Founding Circle Prospectus
Action item — by January 2027
A two-page document that makes the case for private investment in your company. Not a grant application. Not a plea for charity. A clear, confident articulation of what you're building, what it costs, what the investor receives (naming recognition, exclusive access, first tickets), and why this specific project deserves their money. Topher writes the first draft. Cassie edits for emotional truth. Send to 50-100 contacts in your SLC and Chicago networks.
Founders' Discussion

How much of your own money can you commit? What is the number that doesn't cause financial stress in your household? Be honest. Write it down. That number is the floor of Phase 1. Everything above it is what you need to raise.

Module 06
Building an Audience from Zero
Marketing, press, identity, and the long game of trust

You have no audience. You have no mailing list. You have no critical track record in Chicago. This is actually an advantage: you get to build your audience relationship from scratch with no legacy baggage. But it requires a deliberate strategy that most theatre companies never think through.

Read
Building the Successful Theater Company
Lisa Mulcahy — Allworth Press
Interviews with leaders from 19 theatre companies on funding, social media, corporate sponsors, and artistic collaborations. The social media and audience-building chapters are particularly relevant for a company starting from zero brand recognition in a new city.
Study
Rescripted (Formerly Chicago Theatre Review)
rescripted.org
The most comprehensive coverage of Chicago storefront theatre. Read it weekly for the next 20 months. Study what companies get covered, how they're described, what critics respond to. Identify which critics review work most similar to what you want to make. These are the relationships you'll need for October 2027 press outreach.
Bookmark Rescripted →
Study
Chicago Reader — Theatre Section
chicagoreader.com
The Reader covers Chicago's independent arts scene with depth and cultural context that the Tribune often can't match. Its theatre coverage tends to champion the scrappy, the experimental, the politically engaged. Study their feature profiles of emerging companies — the narrative structure of those pieces is the template for how you want to be written about.
Bookmark the Reader →
Do
Build the Brand Identity
Action item — by June 2026
Topher handles this in-house. Company name, domain, logo, social media handles, and a single-page website. The website needs only five things: who you are, what you believe, what you're making, when, and how to get on the mailing list. Every person you meet between now and October 2026 should receive a card or a URL. Start collecting email addresses the day the site goes live. That list is your most valuable asset.
Founders' Discussion

The Thin Place is a 90-minute theatrical séance about a psychic medium. In a 99-seat storefront theatre. In October. How do you market that to people who have never heard of Lucas Hnath and have never heard of you? Write five sentences that would make a stranger buy a ticket.

Module 07
Know Your City
The Chicago theatre ecosystem, venues, institutions, and community

Chicago's theatre community is simultaneously the most welcoming and the most demanding in the country. People will help you — but only if you show up, do the work, and demonstrate that you're here to contribute, not extract. This module is about becoming part of the city before you ask the city to come see your show.

Do
Join the League of Chicago Theatres
Action item — immediately
$150/year for companies with budgets under $25K. Membership gives you ChicagoPlays.com listing, Hot Tix access, cooperative advertising, professional development, and most importantly: a directory of every theatre professional in the city. Attend their workshops, mixers, and events. This is where you meet the people who run the spaces, review the shows, fund the work, and cast the plays.
League of Chicago Theatres →
Visit
See Shows at Your Target Venues
Ongoing — minimum 2 shows/month starting now
See a show at Theatre Wit, Bramble Arts Loft, and The Den Theatre within 60 days. Don't just watch the play — study the space. How does the audience enter? What's the lobby like? How does the seating configuration work? Where do you put the box office? Where do you hang the lights? Talk to the house manager. Introduce yourself to the artistic director after the show. Be a genuine audience member first. Be a potential renter second.
Visit
The Broader Storefront Landscape
Ongoing
See work at Steep Theatre, A Red Orchid, Interrobang, Raven Theatre, Strawdog, Steep, Gift Theatre, and any company producing in the 50-to-100 seat range. These are your peers. These are the companies whose audiences overlap with yours. Note what's working: how do they handle pre-show? Intermission? Post-show? Concessions? Community? These details are the difference between professional and amateur.
Do
Contact Theatre Wit About the Shared Spaces Program
Action item — by April 2026
Theatre Wit received a $600,000 grant from the Paul M. Angell Family Foundation specifically to subsidize rental rates for small companies — from $2,250/week to $1,000/week. This program includes marketing assistance, production guidance, and accessibility services. Contact Jeremy Wechsler, Artistic Director. This venue should be your primary target for the October 2027 production.
Do
Activate Your Bridge Network
Action item — by March 2026
Email Jerry Rapier at Plan-B Theatre and ask for 30 minutes of his time. Email Shannon Musgrave at Carnegie Mellon. Reach out to every SLC collaborator who might have a Chicago connection. Each of these conversations is a potential introduction to the people you need to meet. Don't wait. These conversations should begin now.
Founders' Discussion

After seeing ten shows in Chicago over the next three months: What is the gap? What is the kind of work that nobody is doing that you believe audiences would respond to? Not in theory — based on what you've actually witnessed.

Module 08
Founder Psychology and the Long Game
Burnout, co-founder dynamics, artistic integrity, and the 10-year vision
"The great thing about Steppenwolf is that you keep coming back to do quality work and I think that's one of the major reasons people keep coming back."
Tarell Alvin McCraney, Steppenwolf Ensemble

The hardest part of founding a theatre company is not the first show. It's the third year. It's the Tuesday night when the house is at 30% and you've spent all weekend painting the set and you have work at your day job in seven hours. This module is about sustaining the humans at the center of the enterprise — which means you.

Read
Theatre Management: Arts Leadership for the 21st Century
Anthony Rhine — Allworth Press, 2019
Comprehensive textbook covering the full spectrum of arts organizational leadership: mission development, stakeholder communication, governance, marketing, fund development, and community engagement. Dense but worth it. Read selectively — focus on the chapters about organizational culture, mission articulation, and managing through growth transitions.
Listen
From the Ground Up — Season 3 (Full Season)
Jeffrey Mosser / HowlRound Theatre Commons
The entire third season is a masterclass in ensemble sustainability. Episodes on touring economics, rural practice, funding models, boards, philanthropy, and the history of ensemble-based theatre in America. The Ben Cameron episode on post-pandemic funding shifts and the Bob Leonard episode on the history of the ensemble movement are essential. Listen to the full season over the course of a month.
Subscribe on Apple Podcasts →
Do
Write a Co-Founder Agreement
Action item — by April 2026
Before you incorporate: write down who does what. Who is the artistic director? Who is the managing director? Who makes final casting decisions? Who signs the checks? What happens if one founder wants to leave? What happens if you disagree about a play? Every successful co-founder relationship has answers to these questions before they need them. Put it in writing. It protects the company and it protects the marriage.
Do
Define the 10-Year Vision
Action item — by June 2026
The Thin Place is your first production, not your mission. What kind of company do you want to be in 2037? What does the season look like? How many shows? What size audience? What relationship to new work, to published plays, to the Chicago community? Write a 1-page vision document that you revisit every six months. It will change — that's the point. But you need a compass or you'll wander.
Read
Outrageous Fortune: The Life and Times of the New American Play
Todd London with Ben Pesner & Zannie Voss — TCG, 2009
The most honest accounting of the American theatre's economic and artistic ecosystem ever published. Based on a Theatre Development Fund study surveying hundreds of playwrights, artistic directors, and literary managers. Not a how-to — a mirror. Read it to understand the system you're entering and the pressures that will try to shape you.
Founders' Discussion

It's October 28, 2027. Closing night. You're striking the set at midnight. The run went well — not perfectly, but well. What does success feel like? Not in numbers. In your body. In your relationship. In the knowledge that's been built. Write it down separately, then read each other's versions aloud.
