The Subsidy Trap

Institutional Dependency and the Collapse of American Storefront Theatre
A White Paper on the Structural Economics of Small Professional Theatre
in the United States, 2019–2026

February 2026
Abstract

Between 2019 and 2025, American small and storefront theatre experienced the most severe contraction in its history. Chicago alone lost 60% of its producing companies, dropping from 127 full-season producers to roughly 51. Similar contractions hit Portland, the Bay Area, Minneapolis, Seattle, and Washington, D.C. The conventional explanation cites COVID-19, rising real estate costs, and audience behavior change. These are real factors. But they are symptoms, not the disease.

This paper argues that the primary structural failure is the nonprofit theatre business model itself: a decades-long dependency on institutional philanthropy that has produced organizations incapable of surviving on the value they generate. While every other form of live entertainment in America—music venues, comedy clubs, independent cinemas, bookstores—operates primarily on earned revenue, the average small nonprofit theatre earns only 18–25% of its budget from operations. The remaining 75–82% comes from donations, grants, and government funding. When that contributed revenue contracts, these organizations do not adapt. They die.

The paper examines the contraction in detail, compares theatre’s economics to those of other live entertainment businesses, presents financial models demonstrating what earned-revenue sustainability requires at storefront scale, and proposes that theatre’s future may depend on reimagining the small company not as a charity, but as a business and a piece of essential community cultural infrastructure.

Section 1

Introduction: The Crisis Everyone Can See

In July 2023, Greg Reiner, the National Endowment for the Arts’ director of theater and musical theater, told American Theatre that the agency was seeing “two to three organizations closing a month.”1 By August, Chris Jones of the Chicago Tribune was cataloguing the damage in his own city: Lookingglass Theatre had cut its staff from 24 to 10, Steppenwolf had gone dark on its mainstage for three months mid-season, and at least 34 companies had permanently closed since the pandemic began—a list Jones acknowledged was “not even complete.”2 The Hollywood Reporter documented the national picture: 72 nonprofit theatre institutions outside New York expected to reduce programming by an average of 20% in the 2024 season.3

This is not a story that lacks for telling. The closures have been reported exhaustively. American Theatre, the Chicago Tribune, the Chicago Sun-Times, NPR, PBS, the Hollywood Reporter, and local outlets in every affected city have covered the collapse. What has not been covered—or not with sufficient candor—is why.

The standard account goes something like this: COVID-19 shuttered theaters for 18 months. Some never reopened. Rising rents displaced others. Audiences came back slowly, if at all. Subscriptions collapsed. Donors redirected their giving. Government relief ran out.

All of this is true. None of it is the root cause.

The American small nonprofit theatre was designed to be sustained by charity, and when the charity contracted, the theatre had no business underneath it.

The 501(c)(3) designation that was supposed to protect these organizations instead made them structurally dependent on external subsidy. The grants and donations that were supposed to supplement earned revenue gradually replaced it. And the resulting institutions—undercapitalized, dependent on a few funders, run by exhausted founders working without salary—proved unable to survive even a modest disruption to the philanthropic pipeline.

This paper examines that structural failure. It uses Chicago’s storefront theatre ecosystem as a primary case study—because it is the most documented example, and because its contraction is the most severe—but the argument is national. It compares theatre to other live entertainment businesses that survive without institutional subsidy. It presents financial models demonstrating what sustainability actually requires at the 50–99 seat scale. And it asks whether the future of small American theatre depends on something the sector has resisted for decades: treating the theatre company as a business.

2.2 Chicago: The Case Study

Chicago’s storefront theatre ecosystem offers the most granular view of the contraction because it was the largest, the most documented, and the hardest hit.

Before the pandemic, the Gaylord & Dorothy Donnelley Foundation’s 2019 Storefront Theatre Study surveyed 41 of approximately 250 storefront companies and found they served roughly 267,400 audience members per season and generated $80 million in economic activity. Two-thirds staged world premieres. The League of Chicago Theatres counted more than 200 member companies.6

2.3 Other Cities

The contraction is national, not Chicago-specific:

  • San Francisco Bay Area: Cal Shakes permanently closed after 50 years. Bay Area Children’s Theatre closed abruptly after 19 years. Aurora Theatre Company suspended its 2025–26 season. Oregon Shakespeare Festival’s audience was less than half its 2019 level by September 2022.10
  • Portland: Portland Center Stage launched a $9 million emergency campaign in 2025, warning it would “cease operations” without $2.5 million by August. Artists Repertory Theatre canceled its entire 2023–24 season.11
  • Minneapolis: The Guthrie reported a record $3.8 million deficit in FY2023. The Jungle Theater paused its season mid-year, furloughing all staff, citing a sharp decline in corporate support.12
  • Seattle: Book-It Repertory Theater closed. Northwest Film Forum laid off half its staff.13
  • Washington, D.C.: Source Theatre was sold to restaurateurs for $4.5 million. Constellation Theatre was displaced after 18 years.14

Section 3

The Diagnosis Everyone Gets Wrong

The standard causal account of the contraction ranks roughly as follows: (1) COVID-19 and the incomplete audience recovery, (2) real estate pressure and venue loss, (3) audience behavior change, (4) donor and funder reallocation, (5) leadership burnout. These are all real. A full analysis of each is available in the existing crisis literature—particularly Chris Jones’s Tribune reporting, American Theatre’s “Theatre in Crisis” feature, and the DCASE “Navigating Recovery” report.218

But this diagnosis has a blind spot. It treats the business model as a given and asks what went wrong around it. The more honest question is whether the business model itself is the problem.

Consider: every cause in the standard account is a story about external revenue going away. COVID reduced ticket sales. Real estate increased costs. Audiences stopped subscribing. Donors reallocated. Government relief expired. In each case, the organization was unable to compensate because it had no independent financial engine. It had no reserves. It had no secondary revenue streams. It had no mechanism for generating enough value from its core activity to sustain itself.

This is not a failure of external conditions. It is a failure of design.

18–25%

The Gaylord & Dorothy Donnelley Foundation’s 2019 study found that 71% of Chicago storefront companies relied on contributed income for more than half of their annual revenue.6 The TCG Theatre Facts 2022 report found that the smallest nonprofit theatres (Budget Group 1, under $500,000 annual expenses) earned only 18–25% of their budget from operations.15 The rest—75–82%—came from donations, grants, and government funding.

When an organization derives three-quarters of its revenue from sources unrelated to the value it delivers to its audience, it is not a business. It is a grant-receiving apparatus that produces theatre on the side.

The October 2023 “Decentering Doom” essay in American Theatre pushed back on the crisis narrative, arguing that much of the coverage focused on large institutions led by white cisgender men while ignoring thriving storefronts like Chicago’s Rivendell Theatre, which extended three times and sold out its 47-seat house.16 The essay is correct that not everything is doom. But the structural critique remains: Rivendell survives because it is small enough to function at a low cost base. The companies that died were the ones in the middle—too ambitious for their resources, too dependent on a few funders, and too structurally fragile to absorb any disruption.

Section 4

The Subsidy Trap

4.1 How Dependency Became the Default

The nonprofit model was not always the only option for American theatre. The regional theatre movement of the 1950s and 1960s adopted the 501(c)(3) structure as a way to attract philanthropic support and signal civic purpose. The Ford Foundation’s pivotal grants to regional theatres in the 1960s cemented the model. Over the following decades, the combination of tax-deductible donations, foundation grants, and government arts funding created an ecosystem in which nonprofit status was not just advantageous but assumed.

Diane Ragsdale, who spent six years as a performing arts program officer at the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, directly questioned this assumption in her landmark keynote, “Holding Up the Arts: Can We Sustain What We’ve Created? Should We?”17 The title alone signals the radicalism of the critique: a former Mellon Foundation officer asking whether the entire apparatus of nonprofit arts should be sustained. On her ArtsJournal blog Jumper, Ragsdale wrote about “letting go of the lifestyle to which some arts groups have become accustomed” and argued that the sector’s undercapitalization was not a financial management problem but a structural one.18

Michael Kaiser, former president of the Kennedy Center and turnaround specialist for the Royal Opera House, American Ballet Theatre, and Alvin Ailey, observed in 2020 that “the average museum and performing arts group operates with less than two months of working capital on hand and the average orchestra has a cushion of just 15 days.”19 His colleague Brett Egan noted that cultural organizations “push themselves constantly to the point of exhaustion” and that “the nature is not to sock away capital.”

SMU DataArts’ 2018 Working Capital Report confirmed this with data: theatres averaged approximately one month of working capital—enough to survive 30 days without any income at all. The report found that “undercapitalization was not due to lack of financial fluency among arts organizations. They knew what they needed. Instead, arts organizations found it difficult to ask for the kinds of capital they needed most—cash reserves.”20

In other words: the system was designed to keep organizations permanently dependent on the next grant cycle.

4.3 The Funding Landscape Is Not Recovering

The contributed revenue environment is not merely disrupted; it is structurally shifting:

  • Foundation funding declined 25% in 2024, falling below pre-pandemic levels. The Ford Foundation, Mellon Foundation, and Doris Duke Foundation have all reallocated away from established performing arts organizations. The MAP Fund has ended its national grantmaking program entirely.522
  • Government funding dropped 26% in 2024. In May 2025, the majority of organizations with current NEA grants were told via email that their grants were being terminated effective May 31.23
  • Corporate giving has “plummeted in recent years,” per American Theatre’s Stuart Miller, as companies shift attention toward social and political causes.22
  • Individual giving is concentrating at larger institutions. Small theatres compete for a shrinking pool.5

For organizations that depend on these sources for 75–82% of their revenue, this is not a temporary downturn. It is a structural realignment. The money is not coming back—not at the levels that sustained the pre-pandemic ecosystem.

4.4 The Voices Who Have Been Saying This

This critique is not new. Scott Walters, in Building a Sustainable Theater, argued that theatre became “centralized, bureaucratized, and commoditized” and proposed cooperative ownership and earned-revenue models as alternatives.24 In 2018, a HowlRound contributor wrote “Promise Me You Won’t Start an Arts Nonprofit,” arguing that “every theatre, dance, film, and music artist and/or group should be fiscally sponsored” rather than forming a 501(c)(3).25 The Grantmakers in the Arts published “Please, Don’t Start a Theater Company!” questioning the proliferation of underfunded organizations.26

Devon Smith, debating at the 2014 Americans for the Arts conference, argued that “not only should we allow failing arts organizations to die, we should encourage it.” She noted that for every arts organization that survived between 1990 and 2010, 2.6 more were born—a rate of organizational proliferation that outstripped the available resources to sustain them.27

As theatres grew, the need for fundraising, marketing, and planning developed into a complex institutional structure. These institutional needs became the fixed costs of the theatre, which were difficult to change, while the art and artists became the variable costs.

— “A Revolution in Governance,” HowlRound Theatre Commons

The art became the thing the organization could cut. The fundraising apparatus became the thing it could not.28

Section 5

The Oldest Art Form’s Oldest Argument

5.1 Theatre Has Always Been Dying

Before the structural critique, a historical corrective is necessary: the announcement that theatre is dead or dying is itself a centuries-old tradition. Radio was going to kill it in the 1930s. Television was going to replace it in the 1950s. Film has been displacing it since the early twentieth century. The internet was supposed to finish the job. Streaming was going to deliver the coup de grâce. Each time, the prediction was wrong—not because the competitive threats were imaginary, but because theatre’s core proposition—the live, unrepeatable encounter between performer and audience—is irreplaceable by any recorded medium.

The COVID-19 pandemic came closest. Broadway went dark for 18 months, the longest closure in its history. Across the UK, the Creative Industries Federation projected a £74 billion revenue drop and 400,000 job losses.47 But even this did not kill the form. It killed organizations. There is a difference.

“What is dying in 2026 is not theatre. It is a specific institutional model of theatre delivery—the mid-sized nonprofit sustained by contributed revenue. The art form itself is doing what it has always done: finding the next configuration that works.”

5.2 Grotowski and the Philosophy of Poverty

In 1968, Jerzy Grotowski published Towards a Poor Theatre, one of the most radical theoretical texts in the history of the form. His argument was not about economics. It was about essence. He asked: what can theatre exist without? And he eliminated everything until only one thing remained:

“By gradually eliminating whatever proved superfluous, we found that theatre can exist without make-up, without autonomic costume and scenography, without a separate performance area (stage), without lighting and sound effects, etc. It cannot exist without the actor-spectator relationship of the perpetual, direct, ‘live’ communion.”

— Jerzy Grotowski, Towards a Poor Theatre (1968)48

Grotowski explicitly framed this against what he called “Rich Theatre”—the institutional impulse to respond to competition from film and television by piling on more spectacle, more technology, more borrowed techniques. He called this “artistic kleptomania” and argued it produced “hybrid-spectacles, conglomerates without backbone or integrity.”48 The richer the theatre became, the further it drifted from its essential nature.

His alternative was via negativa—“not a collection of skills but an eradication of blocks.”48 Strip away the sets. Strip away the costumes. Strip away the administrative infrastructure. What remains is an actor and an audience in a room.

The same year, Peter Brook published The Empty Space, opening with what has become the most quoted sentence in modern theatre theory:

“I can take any empty space and call it a bare stage. A man walks across this empty space whilst someone else is watching him, and this is all that is needed for an act of theatre to be engaged.”

— Peter Brook, The Empty Space (1968)49

Brook’s taxonomy divided theatre into four categories. “Deadly Theatre” was the commercial and institutional default—boredom disguised as culture, where “just the right degree of boringness is a reassuring guarantee of a worthwhile event.”49 His antidote was “Rough Theatre”—theatre in back rooms, upstairs rooms, on carts and trestles, “audiences standing, drinking, sitting round tables, audiences joining in, answering back: the torn sheet pinned up across the hall.”49

The relevance to the storefront argument is not incidental. It is foundational. A stripped-down microcompany is not a compromise born of poverty. It is an alignment with the deepest philosophical tradition of the form. The institutional apparatus—the development staff, the board, the grant-compliance infrastructure, the 501(c)(3) filing requirements—these are not theatre. They are the administrative barnacles that accreted around theatre when it adopted the nonprofit institutional model. Grotowski would have called them superfluous. Brook would have called them deadly.

5.3 The Patron Has Always Changed

The structural dependency described in Section 4 is not a new problem. It is a new version of a very old problem: theatre has always required a patron class. What changes across eras is not the need for patronage but the identity of the patron.

In fifth-century Athens, the choregos—a wealthy citizen appointed by the archon—financed the chorus, costumes, and production of dramatic festivals. A tragic choregia cost approximately 3,000 drachmas, roughly ten times what a skilled worker earned in a year. The system was formalized: private wealth funded public art as a civic obligation.50

3,000
drachmas: the cost of a tragic choregia in Athens—ten times a skilled worker’s annual wage. Theatre has always been expensive. The question has always been who pays.

In Elizabethan England, patronage was not merely financial support but legal protection. The Vagrancy Act of 1572 required any performing company to have the patronage of either two judges or one nobleman. Without a patron, actors were legally vagabonds. Shakespeare’s company, the Lord Chamberlain’s Men, existed because Henry Carey provided legal cover as much as financial support.51

In the eighteenth century, David Garrick purchased a share of Drury Lane’s lease for £8,000 and managed it for 29 years, transforming it into the leading theatre in Europe—funded primarily by ticket sales. Playwright Arthur Murphy observed that Britain had four estates: “King, Lords, Commons, and Drury Lane playhouse.”52 This was the emergence of the commercial box-office model: theatre funded by its audience rather than its patron.

In 1962, W. McNeil Lowry of the Ford Foundation awarded $6.1 million to nine nonprofit theaters—“the largest sum ever awarded to theaters by any American philanthropic institution.”53 The Foundation simultaneously established the Theatre Communications Group to professionalize the field. This created the institutional model that has defined American nonprofit theatre for sixty years: the 501(c)(3) sustained by foundation grants, government funding, and individual philanthropy.

The 501(c)(3) model is just the latest patron. And it is withdrawing. The question is not how to bring it back. The question is: who is the patron now?

This paper’s answer is: the audience itself. The people who buy tickets, order drinks, attend workshops, and embed the theatre in their weekly lives. This is not a radical proposition. It is a return to the commercial model that sustained Garrick’s Drury Lane, that sustains every independent music venue and comedy club in America, and that Grotowski and Brook would have recognized as compatible with their vision of what theatre actually needs to survive.

The Comparison Nobody Makes

6.1 Every Other Live Entertainment Business Survives on Earned Revenue

While this paper was being researched, an obvious question kept surfacing: if small-scale live performance is economically unviable, why are there bars with stages on every corner in Chicago? Why did 422 independent bookstores open in 2025 alone?29 Why does the National Independent Venue Association represent 50+ independently owned music venues in Chicago that collectively generate $2.8 billion in annual economic output?30

The answer is not that live music or comedy or books are inherently more valuable than theatre. The answer is that those businesses have revenue models that work.

Nonprofit theatres have become organizations that raise money in order to produce art, rather than organizations that produce art in order to earn money. Every other live entertainment format operates on the inverse model.

6.2 The Bar Is the Business

The most important insight from independent music venue economics is that the show is the loss leader; the bar is the business. NIVA’s 2025 State of Live report found that 64% of independent live stages were not profitable in 2024, despite generating $153.1 billion in aggregate economic output.30 A large percentage of ticket revenue passes directly to artists through guarantees or door splits. The venues that survive do so because bar and beverage sales—operating at 75–80% gross margin—cover the fixed overhead.

Comedy clubs operate on the same principle: food and beverage constitutes 40–60% of total revenue. The two-drink minimum doubles per-patron revenue from approximately $25 to $50+. Successful clubs operate at roughly 20% net margin—significantly higher than restaurants.31

Independent cinemas face a similarly lopsided structure: studios take approximately 50% of ticket revenue, making concessions (at 80%+ gross margin) the survival mechanism. Art House Convergence’s 2024 National Audience Survey found that 43% of arthouse cinema patrons are formal members—a stickiness that most theatres would envy.32

The independent bookstore resurgence is perhaps the most instructive analogue. Since 2020, independent bookstores have grown from 1,916 to 3,218—a 70% increase.29 This is not a book-sales story. It is a community-infrastructure story. As the American Booksellers Association’s CEO described it, independent bookstores are “experiences, community hubs and safe spaces for readers of all ages.” People pay more for books at an independent store because they are buying membership in a community, not just a product.

6.3 What Theatre Can and Cannot Adopt

Theatre faces genuine structural constraints that music venues, comedy clubs, and bookstores do not:

The rehearsal problem. A music venue books a concert on Tuesday for Saturday. The artist arrives, sound-checks for 30 minutes, and performs. A theatre company rehearses for three to six weeks before the first preview, paying artists, renting rehearsal space, and building sets. Zero revenue is generated during this period.

The production cost problem. Each production is a prototype. Unlike a music venue (where the infrastructure is permanent), a theatre company must design and build a unique physical environment for every show: scenic design, costumes, lighting, sound, props. These costs range from $5,000 to $25,000+ per production before any marketing or talent costs.33

The exclusivity problem. A music venue can book 250–350 different acts per year, each bringing its own audience. A theatre company producing three to four shows per year, each running four to six weeks, has three to four chances per year to attract an audience. If one show underperforms, it can sink the entire season.

These are real constraints. But they are not excuses. They define the parameters within which a sustainable model must operate—not reasons to default to a model built on charity.

Section 7

The Third Place Problem

In 1989, sociologist Ray Oldenburg coined the term “third place” to describe social environments separate from home (first place) and work (second place) that are essential to democracy, civic engagement, and sense of belonging. Cafes, bars, barbershops, bookstores, and—in Oldenburg’s explicit framework—theatres.34

Yet most theatre companies do not function as third places. The typical audience experience:

  1. Arrive 15 minutes before curtain.
  2. Sit in the dark for 90 minutes.
  3. Leave.

There is no lingering. No socializing. No reason to be there on a non-performance day. No coffee, no drinks, no community-gathering-space function.

Venue TypeThird Place FunctionDaily ActivationCommunity Identity

Comedy clubBar + classesYes“My improv home”

Steppenwolf’s Front Bar, opened in 2016, offers a model worth studying. Within three years of opening, the combined bar and coffee shop “exceeded all expectations,” becoming a place where ensemble members, visiting artists, and patrons mingle. It serves as a neighborhood hub by day and a social space by night.35 The Bushwick Starr in Brooklyn operates open lobby hours as a community drop-in during the day, with a bar opening before each performance.36 Thalia Hall in Chicago’s Pilsen neighborhood is the gold standard for the multi-use model: a performance space, a restaurant, and two bars in a single building. The venue is the destination; the show is the occasion.37

A theatre company that wants to survive as a business—rather than as a permanent fundraising operation—must solve the third-place problem. The space must have a reason to exist on Tuesday afternoon, not just Saturday at 7:30.

The Math: What Sustainability Actually Requires

8.1 The Break-Even Question

The fundamental economic question for a small professional theatre is: what percentage of seats need to be filled, at what ticket price, to cover what percentage of operating costs from earned revenue alone?

The following analysis assumes 32 performances per production (8 per week over 4 weeks), ticket revenue only (no concessions, no donations), and a moderate production budget of $35,000 (Equity entry-tier, professional design, rented venue).21

Venue Size$25 ticket$30 ticket$35 ticket
50 seats88% occupancy73% occupancy63% occupancy
75 seats58% occupancy49% occupancy42% occupancy
99 seats44% occupancy37% occupancy32% occupancy

The data reveals a sharp constraint: at 50 seats, a $35,000 production at $30 average ticket requires 73% occupancy to break even. That is at the very top of the achievable range, and it only covers production costs—not annual overhead. At 75 seats, the same production requires 49%—well within a healthy range. At 99 seats, it drops to 37%—comfortably achievable even for a first production.

Venue size matters enormously. Moving from 50 to 99 seats nearly halves the required occupancy rate for any given budget. This is the fundamental economic argument for the 75–99 seat venue over the 50-seat black box.

The math is not the obstacle. The model is.

Section 9

Who Is Already Doing This: Chicago Case Studies

The preceding analysis is not purely theoretical. Within Chicago’s own theatre ecosystem, a handful of operators are already building models that look more like businesses than charities. None is a perfect template. But collectively, they demonstrate that earned-revenue-primary theatre is not an abstraction—it is a set of practices already being tested on the ground.

9.1 The Den Theatre: The For-Profit Proving Ground

The Den Theatre Chicago, LLC is the single most important case study in Chicago for the for-profit theatre model. Founded in 2010 by Ryan Martin and Carol Cohen, it began with a single rented room on a second floor in Wicker Park. It now operates five live performance spaces and three bars at 1333 N. Milwaukee Avenue—structured explicitly as an LLC, not a nonprofit.40

Martin’s model runs on three revenue streams: producing theatre and comedy, renting stages to resident companies (five storefront companies currently hold residencies, each committing to a minimum number of productions per year), and food and beverage sales. The bar is not an afterthought; it is the financial engine.

“I produced Bus Stop on a credit card, and no nonprofit board would’ve let me do that. When you don’t have proof you can do something, the only way is to just jump in.”—Ryan Martin, The Den Theatre

He has stated he plans to keep the Den a for-profit organization for the foreseeable future. The Den’s model validates the central argument of this paper: a theatre venue can sustain itself on earned revenue if it activates the space daily, diversifies revenue streams, and treats bar income as structurally essential. Its limitation as a template is that the Den leans heavily on comedy and improv programming alongside straight theatre—a deliberate choice that widens the audience base but shifts the artistic center of gravity.

9.2 Bramble Theatre Company: The Hybrid Structure

Bramble Theatre Company represents the most architecturally intentional hybrid model in Chicago. Founded in 2021 by 19 graduates of the School at Steppenwolf, it opened the Bramble Arts Loft in Andersonville in April 2024—a 101-seat thrust theater, a modular black box, a cabaret stage, and George’s Bar, all housed in the adaptive reuse of the historic Capital Garage building.41

The structural innovation is the separation of entities: Bramble Theatre Company is a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Bramble Arts Loft is a separate for-profit entity that generates revenue from weddings, private events, and space rentals. One hundred percent of the Loft’s profits support the nonprofit theatre company. Co-founder Matthew Lunt explained the logic: “The Arts Loft is a for-profit institution with a flexible mission, we’re open to weddings and events. What’s nice about that is that leaves the rest of the calendar open for a longer production and education programming.”41

$2.8M
Bramble Arts Loft startup cost—against an operating budget of ~$120K

The model is elegant but capital-intensive. This is not a replicable path for most startups. But the structural architecture—for-profit venue cross-subsidizing nonprofit programming—is a template worth studying.

9.3 The Comedy Proof of Concept

iO Theater and The Second City have operated as for-profit businesses in Chicago for decades. Both run shows seven nights a week, operate bars and kitchens, generate significant revenue from training centers, and rent event space. They prove that the for-profit performance venue model works in Chicago at scale.42

The critical insight from comedy is the training pipeline: students become audience, audience becomes performers, performers become instructors. This is an audience-development flywheel that most theatre companies have never built. A theatre company that offered acting classes, playwriting workshops, and open reading series would be replicating the structural advantage that has made improv commercially sustainable since the 1950s.

9.4 The Venue-as-Infrastructure Operators

Several Chicago operators have built business models around the venue itself, independent of any particular artistic program:

The Edge Theater in Edgewater operates as a for-profit rental venue: two performance spaces (104-seat proscenium and 45-seat black box), six total rentable spaces, front-of-house staff, and bar service. The Edge does not produce art. It provides infrastructure for others to produce art—and it pays its rent doing it.43

Rhapsody Theater in Rogers Park, owned by Dr. Ricardo T. Rosenkranz, operates as a for-profit entertainment destination in the renovated historic Morse Theater building. Its 200-seat venue programs magic, cabaret, chamber music, world music, and dance—a deliberate breadth that keeps the room filled. Bar and food service and private event rentals supplement ticket sales.44

Theater Wit is a nonprofit, but it functions in part as a venue landlord: artistic director Jeremy Wechsler operates three 99-seat black-box spaces in Lakeview and rents them to resident companies including Shattered Globe, Remy Bumppo, and Strawdog at $2,250 per week (recently reduced to $1,000 through a $600,000 grant from the Walder Foundation).45 It is Wechsler who has documented the contraction most precisely—127 companies to 51, 19 stages lost—and his data anchors Section 2 of this paper.

9.5 The Third Track: L.C. Bernadine and Independent Productions

Playwright L.C. Bernadine has launched the Independent Productions Initiative through Redtwist Theatre as fiscal agent—a fund that explicitly supports productions “not programmed or sponsored by a theater company with 501(c)(3) tax status.”46 In its first year, IPI raised nearly $140,000 and distributed 23 grants ranging from $500 to $5,000.

$140K
Raised in year one by the Independent Productions Initiative—23 grants outside the 501(c)(3) apparatus

Bernadine describes this as a “third track” of Chicago theatre: neither the large institutions nor the storefront companies, but aimed at “creative folks with a big vision but no interest in running a theater.” The philosophical premise is significant: that the future of Chicago theatre may not be more companies at all, but more productions—individual projects funded, produced, and completed outside the nonprofit institutional apparatus entirely.

9.6 The Gap

What is notable about this landscape is the gap at the center. The Den does it, but tilts toward comedy and variety programming. Bramble is structurally innovative but required $2.8 million in startup capital. The Edge and Rhapsody are venue operators, not producing companies. iO and Second City are comedy institutions with 40–65 years of brand equity.

No one in Chicago is currently operating a small, for-profit, legitimate-theatre-producing LLC with the artistic ambition of an A Red Orchid or a Steep but the business discipline of The Den. No company is running on earned-revenue primacy while producing serious drama at professional quality in the 50–99 seat range. The model described in this paper’s financial analysis—an LLC producing two to three shows per year, operating at 53% occupancy in a 75-seat house, covering 100% of a $77,000 budget from ticket sales alone—exists on paper. It does not yet exist in practice.

That is the opportunity.

Section 10

The 2026 Moment

10.1 The Political and Institutional Climate

This paper is being written in February 2026, in the early months of a second Trump administration that has already demonstrated its posture toward public arts funding. In May 2025, the majority of organizations holding current NEA grants were told via email that their grants were being terminated.23 The broader federal posture toward institutional arts support is one of indifference at best and hostility at worst.

But the political moment is only the most visible expression of a deeper shift. Foundation funding declined 25% in 2024. Corporate giving has “plummeted.”22 Government funding dropped 26%.5 The institutional infrastructure that sustained the nonprofit theatre model for sixty years is not experiencing a temporary disruption. It is experiencing a structural realignment. Companies that plan on the assumption that the pre-pandemic funding landscape will return are planning for a future that does not exist.

In this environment, the earned-revenue-primary model is not merely a strategic preference. It is a survival requirement. The organizations least exposed to institutional instability are the ones that never depended on institutions in the first place.

10.2 People Are Hungry

The demand for live experience is not declining. It is exploding.

$2.08B
Taylor Swift’s Eras Tour gross—the first $2 billion concert tour in history. 149 sold-out dates. 10.17 million attendees.54

Live Nation reported $23 billion in revenue in 2024 with 151 million fans across 50,000+ events, both records.55 The top 10 comedy touring acts grossed a combined $396.7 million, a 52% increase from 2023.56 The NFL, NBA, NHL, MLS, and NWSL all hit record attendance. The global live entertainment market is valued at $202.9 billion and projected to reach $270 billion by 2030. The experience economy as a whole is projected at $2.1 trillion by 2032.57

$23B
Live Nation’s 2024 revenue. 151 million fans. 50,000+ events. Both all-time records. People are not staying home.55

People are not staying home. They are going out. They are spending record sums on shared live experiences. They are choosing concerts over products, games over goods. Seventy percent of Gen Z respondents say they would sacrifice retail purchases to fund experiential outings.57

And yet: NEA data shows that non-musical theatre attendance has declined from 11.9% to 4.5% of U.S. adults over forty years—a 62% drop. Musical theatre attendance fell from 18.6% to 10.3%.58 Even Broadway, which set a grossing record of $1.89 billion in the 2024–2025 season, saw its number of unique theatergoers decline by 330,000, and suburban attendance hit its lowest level in 30 years.59

“The problem is not that audiences have lost their appetite for live experiences. The problem is that theatre has lost its connection to the audiences who are spending.”

10.3 The Generation That Doesn’t Know the Canon

There is an upcoming generation of potential audience members that has no inherited relationship to the theatrical canon and no loyalty to existing institutions. This is not a problem. It is an opportunity.

51%
of Gen Z adults attend live theater at least every few months—compared to only 19% of adults over 55. The youngest generation is the most engaged with live performance.60

CivicScience survey data from 2024 found that 51% of Gen Z adults attend live theater at least every few months—compared to only 19% of adults over 55.60 A Broadway League survey found that nearly 70% of Gen Z and Millennials describe themselves as “Broadway Curious”—but 40% say they have never seen anything about Broadway in their social media feeds.61 They prefer experiences over products. They do not respond to discounts, which they read as quality signals. They have built their own canon through TikTok virality rather than through institutional channels or critical authority.

Trevor Boffone’s TikTok Broadway: Musical Theatre Fandom in the Digital Age (Oxford University Press, 2024) documents how social media is “creating a new canon of musical theatre that reflects the preferences and passions of the fans” rather than the programming decisions of institutions.62 The Wicked film grossed $758.8 million worldwide on the back of precisely this cross-platform momentum.

For a new storefront company, this generation represents a clean-slate audience. They have no expectation of how theatre “should” work. They have no subscription habit to break. They have no loyalty to any institution. They are waiting to be reached by a company that speaks their language, prices its work honestly, and treats the experience as something worth the money—because they already believe live experiences are worth the money. They just have not been given a reason to spend it here.

10.4 AI and the Microcompany

In the mid-2010s, a small nonprofit theatre company needed at minimum an artistic director, a managing director, a marketing/communications person, and a development staffer—three to five people before a single production was mounted. In 2026, artificial intelligence has compressed the marketing, communications, development, and administrative functions to the point where two founders can plausibly cover the workload that previously required a staff of five.

A TechSoup survey of 1,321 nonprofit professionals found that over 60% of organizations with budgets under $1 million are already exploring AI for grant writing, donor outreach, and administrative automation. Twenty-five percent are actively using it for grant writing. Foundation program officers are “actively encouraging AI adoption, especially for smaller, resource-limited nonprofits.”63

The World Economic Forum has described this phenomenon as enabling the “one-person unicorn”—the premise that generative AI “is making sophisticated technology and specialized skills more broadly accessible, empowering small teams to achieve more.”64

The implication for storefront theatre is direct: the administrative infrastructure that justified the 501(c)(3)—the development staff, the marketing coordinator, the grant writer—can now be handled by tools. This does not eliminate the need for human judgment, artistic vision, or relational work. It eliminates the need for a staff to perform the rote functions that consumed a disproportionate share of a small company’s budget and attention.

10.5 No Margin, No Mission

There is a critique to be anticipated. The argument for earned-revenue primacy, for treating a theatre company as a business, for rejecting structural dependence on philanthropy—this can read, from the radical progressive perspective, as capitalistic, individualistic, and ethically suspect. Art, in this view, should not be subject to market forces. The commodification of culture is itself a harm. The correct response to the funding crisis is to demand more funding, not to accept its absence.

This paper takes the critique seriously but rejects its conclusion.

The choice facing a storefront theatre maker in 2026 is not between purity and commerce. It is between making work and not making work. If the subsidy is not coming—and the data in Sections 2 through 4 argues persuasively that it is not, at the levels that sustained the pre-pandemic ecosystem—then the options are: (a) wait for it, (b) give up, or (c) build something that sustains itself.

Option (a) is what the sector has been doing for three years. The result is a 60% contraction. Option (b) is what 76 Chicago companies have already chosen, whether voluntarily or not. Option (c) is the subject of this paper.

“No margin, no mission” contains a truth that the sector has been reluctant to internalize: an organization that cannot sustain itself financially cannot sustain its artistic mission. The most radical thing a theatre company can do in 2026 is not to write a better grant application. It is to build a business model that does not require one.

This is not a concession to capitalism. It is a refusal to be dependent on the generosity of institutions that are themselves retreating. It is an assertion that the work has value—measurable, exchangeable value—and that the people who experience it should be willing to pay for it. If they are not, that is information. And if they are, that is a business.

Section 11

Toward a Model

11.1 What the Data Says Works

The companies that survived the 2019–2025 contraction share common characteristics. They are either institutionally robust (Steppenwolf, with its $16 million budget, 50-year brand, and endowment, has contracted but survived) or authentically lean (Rivendell, which sold out its 47-seat house and extended three times; A Red Orchid, which has thrived for 33 years at 65 seats).2

The danger zone is the middle: $200K–$800K annual budget, 3–5 staff, a leased space, dependence on 2–3 foundation grants, and a subscription model. This is where organizations are too big to be scrappy and too small to be institutional. This is where the death toll was highest.

The companies that are emerging—Bramble Theatre Company in Chicago operates with both nonprofit and for-profit status, using its Bramble Arts Loft as a revenue-generating venue38—are building differently from the start.

11.2 The LLC + Fiscal Sponsorship Path

The HowlRound essay “Promise Me You Won’t Start an Arts Nonprofit” made the case directly: fiscal sponsorship through organizations like Fractured Atlas (currently sponsoring over 4,000 projects nationwide) provides a tax-deductible donation channel without the costs, compliance burden, or governance requirements of 501(c)(3) status.25

An LLC paired with fiscal sponsorship offers:

  • No board of directors. No board means no board dysfunction—the governance problem that contributed to the collapse of Victory Gardens and others.
  • No 990 filings, no grant compliance infrastructure. The administrative overhead that the traditional model requires is real: $5,000–$15,000 per year in grant writing, reporting, and accounting, plus the opportunity cost of the artistic director’s time.
  • Earned-revenue primacy by default. Without the grant-writing apparatus, the company must generate revenue from its operations. This is a feature, not a bug.
  • Graceful failure. If the company does not work, it dissolves cleanly. It does not accumulate institutional obligations, board commitments, or funder relationships that create pressure to continue past the point of viability.

The 501(c)(3) may make sense eventually—if and when the company reaches a scale where the tax advantages and grant access justify the overhead. For a startup, the evidence suggests it is a trap.

11.3 Theatre as Community Infrastructure

The independent bookstore resurgence offers the most instructive parallel for theatre’s future. Bookstores are not experiencing a resurgence because Amazon stopped selling books. They are experiencing a resurgence because they solved the community-infrastructure problem. They became third places. They became the neighborhood’s living room. People go there on Tuesday afternoon to browse and have coffee, not just on Saturday when their favorite author is reading.

The utilitarian argument for theatre is not that it is art and art is important. The utilitarian argument is that a permanent stage in a neighborhood is a public good: a place where people gather, where local stories are told, where the community sees itself reflected. This is the same argument that sustains libraries, community centers, and parks. It does not depend on philanthropic sentiment. It depends on the space earning its place in the neighborhood’s daily life.

This requires theatres to think of themselves differently—not as producers of art who need the community’s charity, but as community assets that produce art as one of several functions. The bar. The classes. The workshops. The open-lobby hours. The reading series. The neighborhood event space. All of these generate revenue, build community, and create the kind of relationship with an audience that a four-week run every three months cannot.

11.4 What Has to Change

The sector’s conversation has been dominated by a question of survival: how do we keep what we have? The data suggests a different question is more productive: what would we build if we were starting from scratch, knowing what we now know?

The answer involves several departures from the norms of the last 30 years:

  1. Earned-revenue primacy. The target is 70–80% earned revenue, not 18–25%. This means ticket pricing, bar/concessions, workshops, classes, rentals, and event programming must be central to the business plan, not afterthoughts.
  2. Daily activation. The space must be open and generating revenue or community value more than four hours on a Saturday. This may mean partnering with a food/beverage operator, running daytime programming, hosting community events, or simply creating a space people want to be in.
  3. Low fixed costs. The itinerant model—renting space per production rather than leasing a permanent venue—eliminates the 12-month overhead commitment that killed many companies when revenue dropped. A permanent space should be earned through sustained demand, not assumed from the start.
  4. Multiple revenue streams. A company that depends on ticket sales alone is nearly as fragile as one that depends on grants alone. The most resilient models combine tickets, bar/concessions, classes, events, rentals, and modest individual giving.
  5. Structural independence from philanthropy. Donations are welcome. Grants, if they materialize, are welcome. But the company must be designed to survive without them. This is the fundamental shift.

Seth Gordon’s Crossroads in the American Theatre asks the right question: “How did the nonprofit resident theatre movement, originally created as an alternative to Broadway, grow into a system that itself cries out for transformative change?”39 The answer, this paper suggests, is that the system was built on a revenue model that required constant external subsidy, and when the subsidy contracted, the system had nothing to fall back on.

The theatre that survives the next decade will not be the one that writes the best grant applications. It will be the one that fills enough seats, pours enough drinks, teaches enough classes, and embeds itself deeply enough in its neighborhood that the community sustains it through commerce—because it has earned that sustenance, not because it has asked for it.
